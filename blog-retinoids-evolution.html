Retinoids: From Acne Treatment to Cornerstone of Modern Dermatology

Dr. Quoc-Bao Nguyen • January 12, 2026 • 8 min read

Few classes of medications have shaped dermatology as profoundly as retinoids. Originally developed to treat acne, retinoids have evolved into foundational therapies for photoaging, pigmentary disorders, and certain skin cancers. Their longevity is not accidental - it reflects a deep and expanding understanding of skin biology.

The Early Era: Vitamin A and Acne

The story of retinoids begins with vitamin A and its role in epithelial differentiation. In the 1960s and 1970s, topical tretinoin was introduced for acne, demonstrating an ability to normalize follicular keratinization and reduce comedone formation.

This marked a turning point. For the first time, acne could be treated by modifying the underlying biology of the skin rather than simply suppressing inflammation or bacteria.

Expansion Beyond Acne

Over time, researchers observed additional effects: improved skin texture, reduced fine lines, and more even pigmentation. Retinoids were found to stimulate collagen production, accelerate epidermal turnover, and improve dermal organization.

These discoveries expanded retinoid use into:

  • Photoaging and wrinkles
  • Dyschromia and uneven tone
  • Actinic damage and field cancerization

Retinoids became one of the few treatments supported by decades of clinical evidence across multiple indications.

Refinement and Tolerability

As efficacy became well established, the next challenge was tolerability. Early formulations were effective but often irritating, limiting adherence.

This led to the development of newer molecules and delivery systems:

  • Adapalene and tazarotene with improved receptor selectivity
  • Controlled-release and micronized formulations
  • Lower-strength options suitable for long-term use

The goal shifted from short-term correction to sustainable, lifelong skin health.

Over-the-Counter and Medical Grade Convergence

In recent years, retinoids have moved beyond prescription-only use. Adapalene became available over the counter, and cosmetic formulations of retinol and retinaldehyde gained popularity.

While not equivalent to prescription tretinoin, these products broadened access and introduced retinoids to a wider audience - often earlier in life, where long-term benefit is greatest.

What’s New: Modern Retinoid Science

Current innovation focuses less on discovering entirely new molecules and more on improving delivery, tolerability, and precision.

Key areas of development include:

  • Encapsulated and time-release retinoids
  • Combination formulations that reduce irritation
  • Targeted receptor modulation to limit side effects

These advances aim to preserve efficacy while expanding who can safely use retinoids.

What’s Upcoming: The Next Phase

The future of retinoids lies in personalization and integration:

  • Retinoids paired with barrier-supporting agents
  • Formulations designed for sensitive skin and skin of color
  • AI-assisted regimen design to optimize dosing and sequencing

Rather than a single “stronger” retinoid, the next generation emphasizes smarter use.

A Timeless Class, Still Evolving

Retinoids remain unmatched in their ability to influence skin biology at a fundamental level. Few therapies can claim the same breadth of evidence or longevity.

Their evolution reflects a larger truth in dermatology: progress is not always about replacement. Sometimes, it is about refinement.

Used correctly, retinoids are not a trend. They are a cornerstone.
