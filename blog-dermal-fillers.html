Dermal Fillers and Fat Transfer: Volume Restoration

Dr. Quoc-Bao Nguyen • December 3, 2025 • 8 min read

Volume loss is a central feature of facial aging. Over time, bone resorption, fat compartment atrophy, and soft-tissue descent change facial contours and proportions. Modern aesthetic medicine offers two primary tools to address this loss: dermal fillers and fat transfer. While both aim to restore volume, they differ fundamentally in mechanism, durability, and appropriate use.

Dermal Fillers: Precision and Reversibility

Dermal fillers—most commonly hyaluronic acid–based—allow for precise, controlled volume replacement. They are injectable, adjustable, and, in many cases, reversible.

Fillers are particularly useful for:

  • Targeted contour refinement
  • Subtle volume restoration
  • Areas requiring predictability and flexibility
  • Patients seeking minimal downtime

Because fillers can be layered and adjusted over time, they are well suited for incremental change and maintenance.

Fat Transfer: Structural Restoration

Fat transfer involves harvesting a patient's own fat, processing it, and reinjecting it into areas of volume loss. Unlike fillers, fat transfer is both volumizing and biologically active.

Potential advantages include:

  • Use of autologous tissue
  • Long-term volume retention in selected patients
  • Improvement in skin quality in some cases

However, fat survival is variable, and results depend on technique, recipient site, and patient-specific factors.

Durability and Predictability

Fillers provide predictable, time-limited results. Their temporary nature can be an advantage, allowing adaptation as facial structure continues to change with age.

Fat transfer offers the possibility of longer-lasting correction but with less immediate predictability. Some resorption is expected, and secondary procedures may be required.

Choosing between the two is not a matter of permanence versus impermanence—it is a matter of control versus integration.

Safety and Technique Matter

Both fillers and fat transfer are highly technique-dependent. Vascular anatomy, depth of injection, and product selection directly affect outcomes and risk.

Serious complications are rare but real, emphasizing the importance of:

  • Advanced anatomical knowledge
  • Conservative dosing
  • Clear treatment planning

Volume restoration should enhance natural structure—not overwhelm it.

Not Competing, but Complementary

Fillers and fat transfer are not opposing treatments. In many cases, they are complementary. The best results come from selecting the right tool for the right problem—not forcing one solution to fit all faces.

A Thoughtful Approach to Facial Aging

Effective volume restoration respects proportion, symmetry, and individual anatomy. The goal is not to recreate a younger face, but to preserve balance and expression over time.

Whether using filler, fat transfer, or both, outcomes depend on judgment more than material.

Restraint remains the most valuable aesthetic skill.

Dermal Fillers Used in Aesthetic Dermatology

Hyaluronic Acid (HA) Fillers

Most commonly used fillers; temporary, adjustable, and reversible.

  • Juvéderm Ultra / Ultra Plus
  • Juvéderm Voluma
  • Juvéderm Volbella
  • Juvéderm Vollure
  • Restylane
  • Restylane Lyft
  • Restylane Defyne
  • Restylane Refyne
  • Restylane Kysse
  • Belotero Balance

Common uses: lips, cheeks, nasolabial folds, tear troughs, jawline, fine lines.

Calcium Hydroxylapatite (CaHA) Fillers

Thicker fillers that provide structure and collagen stimulation.

  • Radiesse

Common uses: jawline, chin, cheeks, hand rejuvenation. Not typically used in lips or superficial areas.

Poly-L-Lactic Acid (PLLA) Fillers

Biostimulatory fillers that stimulate collagen over time.

  • Sculptra

Common uses: global volume restoration, temples, cheeks, jawline. Results develop gradually over months.

Polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) Fillers

Permanent fillers used selectively.

  • Bellafill

Used in specific indications with careful patient selection.

Fat Transfer (Autologous Fat Grafting)

Fat transfer uses a patient's own fat to restore volume.

Common treatment areas:

  • Cheeks
  • Temples
  • Under-eye hollows
  • Jawline
  • Nasolabial folds
  • Acne scars

Fat is harvested (often from abdomen or thighs), processed, and reinjected. Results can be long-lasting, though some resorption is expected.
