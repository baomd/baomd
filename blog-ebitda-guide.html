Practice Valuation: Understanding EBITDA for Private Practitioners

Dr. Quoc-Bao Nguyen • January 2026 • 8 min read

At some point, most private practitioners hear the same phrase: “Your practice is valued at a multiple of EBITDA.” For many physicians, EBITDA feels abstract—an accounting term used by investors rather than clinicians. In reality, EBITDA is simply a way to measure how efficiently a practice generates cash from its core operations.

Understanding EBITDA allows physicians to see their practice the way buyers do—and to make better decisions long before a sale is ever on the table.

What Is EBITDA?

EBITDA stands for Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. In practical terms, it represents the cash flow produced by a practice before financing structure and non-operating accounting items.

For a medical practice, EBITDA answers one question:

How much cash does this business generate from patient care after normal operating expenses?

Why EBITDA Matters in Practice Valuation

Buyers—whether private equity groups, hospital systems, or strategic partners—use EBITDA because it allows them to compare practices on equal footing.

EBITDA:

Removes differences in physician tax strategies

Ignores personal debt or ownership structure

Focuses on operational performance

Valuation is typically expressed as a multiple of EBITDA, not revenue.

How EBITDA Is Calculated in a Medical Practice

At a high level:

Revenue − Operating expenses (staff, rent, supplies, billing, malpractice, IT, etc.) = EBITDA

However, physician practices require adjustments.

The Key Adjustment: Physician Compensation

For owner-physicians, compensation must be “normalized.”

If an owner pays themselves above or below market rates, EBITDA is adjusted to reflect what it would cost to replace them with a similarly trained clinician.

This normalization is critical. Without it, EBITDA is distorted.

Common EBITDA Add-Backs

Add-backs are expenses that reduce reported profit but are not considered part of normal ongoing operations.

Examples may include:

Excess owner compensation

Personal expenses run through the practice

One-time legal or consulting fees

Non-recurring equipment purchases

Add-backs must be reasonable and defensible.

What Drives EBITDA Multiples

Not all EBITDA is valued equally. Multiples vary based on:

Specialty and procedure mix

Growth trajectory

Payer mix and reimbursement stability

Provider concentration risk

Operational maturity and scalability

A well-run practice with predictable cash flow commands a higher multiple.

Why Revenue Alone Is Misleading

High revenue does not guarantee high value. Two practices with identical revenue can have dramatically different EBITDA—and therefore very different valuations.

Efficiency, not volume, drives value.

Improving EBITDA Before a Transaction

Practices improve valuation by:

Standardizing operations

Optimizing staffing and scheduling

Improving billing efficiency and collections

Reducing unnecessary overhead

Diversifying revenue streams

These changes often improve income even if a sale never occurs.

Think Like an Owner, Not Just a Clinician

EBITDA is not just for selling. It is a tool for understanding the financial health of your practice.

Physicians who understand EBITDA:

Negotiate better deals

Plan growth more effectively

Maintain control over their professional future

Final Perspective

EBITDA is not an abstract investor concept. It is a clear lens on how your practice performs as a business.

Whether or not you plan to sell, understanding EBITDA allows you to make decisions with intention—not surprise.

A well-run practice is valuable. EBITDA is how that value is measured.
