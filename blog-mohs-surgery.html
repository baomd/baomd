Mohs Micrographic Surgery: Precision Skin Cancer Treatment

Dr. Quoc-Bao Nguyen • January 20, 2026 • 7 min read

Mohs micrographic surgery is a specialized technique for treating certain skin cancers with the highest possible cure rate while preserving as much healthy tissue as possible. It is particularly valuable for cancers on the face and other anatomically or functionally sensitive areas.

The procedure combines staged surgical removal with immediate microscopic examination of the tissue margins, allowing for complete cancer clearance before reconstruction begins.

What Makes Mohs Surgery Different

Unlike standard excision, Mohs surgery evaluates 100% of the surgical margin in real time. Cancer is removed in thin layers, each carefully mapped and examined under a microscope. Additional tissue is removed only if cancer cells remain.

This approach offers two major advantages:

  • Maximum cure rates
  • Minimal removal of healthy tissue

The result is a procedure that balances oncologic safety with functional and cosmetic preservation.

When Mohs Surgery Is Recommended

Mohs surgery is commonly used for:

  • Basal cell carcinoma
  • Squamous cell carcinoma
  • Recurrent or previously treated skin cancers
  • Tumors with aggressive growth patterns
  • Cancers located on the face, scalp, ears, hands, feet, or genital area

Not every skin cancer requires Mohs surgery. The decision depends on multiple factors, including tumor type, location, size, prior treatment, and patient-specific considerations. Careful selection is essential.

What to Expect on the Day of Surgery

Mohs surgery is performed in an outpatient setting using local anesthesia.

The process typically includes:

  1. Removal of the visible tumor
  2. Microscopic examination of the tissue
  3. Additional stages as needed until margins are clear
  4. Reconstruction once the cancer is fully removed

Because the number of stages varies, the length of the visit cannot be precisely predicted. Patients should plan to spend several hours in the office.

Reconstruction After Mohs Surgery

Once the cancer is cleared, attention turns to reconstruction. This may involve:

  • Allowing the wound to heal naturally
  • Layered closure
  • Local tissue rearrangement
  • Skin grafting, when appropriate

Reconstruction is planned with an emphasis on restoring normal contour, function, and appearance - not simply closing the wound. Decisions are individualized and based on anatomy, healing dynamics, and long-term outcomes.

Cure Rates and Outcomes

Mohs surgery offers the highest cure rates available for many common skin cancers:

  • Up to 99% for primary basal cell carcinoma
  • Up to 94-97% for recurrent tumors

Equally important are functional and cosmetic outcomes, which depend on thoughtful surgical planning and reconstruction.

A Measured, Patient-Centered Approach

Mohs surgery is a powerful tool - but not a default. Each recommendation is made with careful consideration of risks, benefits, and alternatives. When Mohs surgery is chosen, it is because it offers a clear advantage for long-term health and quality of life.

Scheduling a Consultation

If you have been diagnosed with skin cancer or referred for Mohs surgery, a consultation allows for review of pathology, discussion of treatment options, and development of an individualized surgical plan.
