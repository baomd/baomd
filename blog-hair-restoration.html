Hair Restoration: Medical Therapy, Supplements, and Procedural Options

Dr. Quoc-Bao Nguyen • January 11, 2026 • 9 min read

Hair loss is common, progressive, and biologically driven. While it often presents as a cosmetic concern, effective treatment requires medical understanding, early intervention, and long-term planning. Modern hair restoration is not about a single product or procedure—it is about stabilization first, restoration second.

Understanding Hair Loss

The most common cause of hair loss in both men and women is androgenetic alopecia, a genetically determined process driven by follicular sensitivity to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). Other causes include:

  • Telogen effluvium (stress, illness, hormonal shifts)
  • Alopecia areata (autoimmune)
  • Scarring alopecias
  • Nutritional deficiencies or systemic disease

Accurate diagnosis is essential. Treatments that work for androgenetic alopecia will not be effective for inflammatory or scarring conditions.

Medical Therapy: The Foundation of Hair Restoration

Medical therapy is the cornerstone of treatment. Its primary goal is to slow or stop progression, which is essential before considering any procedural intervention.

Minoxidil (Topical and Oral)

Minoxidil prolongs the growth (anagen) phase of the hair cycle and increases follicular size.

  • Topical minoxidil (2% or 5%) is available over the counter
  • Low-dose oral minoxidil may be prescribed in select patients

Minoxidil does not address hormonal causes of hair loss, but it improves density and thickness when used consistently. Results typically appear after 3–6 months.

Finasteride

Finasteride reduces conversion of testosterone to DHT by inhibiting 5-alpha reductase.

  • Available as an oral prescription
  • Also used in topical formulations

Finasteride is one of the most effective treatments for male-pattern hair loss and is sometimes used off-label in women under medical supervision. It works best when started early and used long term.

Dutasteride

Dutasteride inhibits both type I and type II 5-alpha reductase enzymes, resulting in more complete DHT suppression.

  • Prescription-only
  • Used selectively in patients with progressive or refractory hair loss

Because of its potency, patient selection and counseling are critical.

Over-the-Counter Supplements and Adjuncts

Nutrafol and Viviscal

Nutraceuticals such as Nutrafol and Viviscal aim to support hair growth by addressing:

  • Nutritional deficiencies
  • Stress and inflammation
  • Hormonal balance

While supplements alone do not reverse androgenetic alopecia, they may provide benefit as adjunctive therapy, particularly in women or patients with diffuse shedding.

Ketoconazole Shampoo

Ketoconazole-containing shampoos may reduce scalp inflammation and DHT activity locally. They are commonly used as supportive therapy alongside medical treatment.

Procedural Options

Platelet-Rich Plasma (PRP)

PRP involves injecting concentrated growth factors derived from a patient’s own blood into the scalp. It may:

  • Improve hair thickness
  • Reduce shedding
  • Support follicular health

PRP works best when combined with medical therapy and is not a substitute for it.

Hair Transplantation

Hair transplantation redistributes genetically resistant follicles to areas of thinning.

Successful outcomes depend on:

  • Donor hair quality
  • Stability of hair loss
  • Surgical planning and technique

Transplantation restores hair but does not stop future loss—ongoing medical therapy remains essential.

Hair Restoration in Women

Hair loss in women often presents as diffuse thinning and may involve hormonal, inflammatory, or metabolic contributors. Treatment emphasizes:

  • Medical therapy and scalp health
  • Nutritional and hormonal evaluation
  • Conservative procedural intervention when appropriate

A tailored approach is critical.

Timing and Expectations

The most common mistake in hair restoration is waiting too long. Early intervention preserves follicles and improves long-term outcomes.

Key expectations:

  • Results take months, not weeks
  • Maintenance is lifelong
  • Not all hair loss is reversible

Consistency matters more than intensity.

A Long-Term Strategy

Effective hair restoration relies on:

  • Accurate diagnosis
  • Evidence-based medical therapy
  • Thoughtful use of supplements and procedures
  • Long-term maintenance

There is no shortcut that replaces biology.

The Dermatologic Perspective

Hair loss is a medical condition with cosmetic consequences—not the other way around. When treated early and systematically, many patients achieve meaningful improvement and long-term stability.

The goal is not maximal density at any cost, but natural, sustainable results over time.
